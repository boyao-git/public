I am a curious tinkerer. I am curious just about everything and will give most things a try to understand it a bit more. If I happened to like something, I will keep tinkering with it until it becomes mine.

My daily routine involves solving problems, building stuff, analysing data, and pondering ideas. I specialise in cognitive neuroscience of language. My current research interest is all about that little voice you hear in your head (a form of mental self talk or verbal imagery) when you think, read and memorise verbal information. The voices in our heads are private and mysterious. Why do we talk in our heads? How does the brain create voices out of nowhere? There are various theories trying to explain how inner voices might be produced but none is conclusive. I am on a quest to figure out how exactly inner voices are created by distinct neuronal firing patterns in the cortex.

My other research interest is about concepts. Language is a symbolic system that indexes our experiences. While concrete concepts (e.g., a cat) are meaningfully linked to our sensory experiences (e.g., what we see, hear and feel), how abstract concepts (e.g., truth) are mentally represented intrigues me. People have made several proposals as to what constitute abstract meaning but they either apply to a subset of concepts or are not testable experimentally. I think the key to this problem is the context in which abstract words are used. I attempt to explore this problem by varying task demands and with a crosslinguistic approach. If you are curious about my research, or want to look through my papers, you are welcome to have a quick browse through my work page or lab page.
